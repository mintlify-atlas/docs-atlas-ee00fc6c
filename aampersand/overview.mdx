aampersand (stylized as **&mpersand**) is a writing tool for fiction writers who have outgrown their documents. It's built for the writer who is 44 chapters in, juggling a dozen plotlines, and no longer certain what they've already written.

The core idea: **the story is the truth. The document is just one expression of it.**

Most writing tools treat the document as the source of reality. aampersand inverts this. The story lives in the writer's head; the tool's job is to surface its structure — plotlines, character threads, narrative beats — so the writer can see it clearly without having to hold all of it at once.

## The Beats system

A Beat is a claim about your narrative. Not a comment, not a highlight — a database record that says:

> "Right here, the reader learns X."

> "Right here, the love interest has dug himself into a hole."

Unlike annotations in Word or Google Docs that are attached to text and die when that text changes, Beats are independent records. The text can drift, the text can disappear — the Beat survives and tells you about it.

### Beat states

| State | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Healthy** | Beat is anchored to text and the text still matches. |
| **Stale** | The anchor exists but the text inside has changed. The system flags it with a gentle note. |
| **Ghost** | The anchored text was deleted. The Beat is caught before it falls into the void — metadata is preserved, document location is stripped, and it surfaces in the Ghost Tray for the writer to re-anchor. |

### Beat lifecycle

```plaintext
HEALTHY → (text edited) → STALE → (text deleted) → GHOST → (writer re-anchors) → HEALTHY
```

The Ghost Tray is where orphaned Beats wait. The writer sees what they had, decides whether it still belongs, and re-anchors it to new text — or discards it intentionally.

## Design evolution: from multi-pane to two-pane

The first version of aampersand had a VS Code-inspired multi-pane layout. Taylor is a developer who loves VS Code, and the surface-level problem looked similar: navigate a large body of interconnected files.

It didn't work.

> Interface patterns don't port across domains — even when the surface-level problem looks the same.

The breakdown came from a fundamental difference in how developers and writers navigate:

| | Developers | Writers |
|---|---|---|
| **Navigate by** | File location | Narrative position |
| **Mental model** | The bug could be anywhere | The story is in my head |
| **Need to see** | Everything, simultaneously | Less — context on demand |
| **Overhead risk** | Missing a relevant file | Managing the layout itself |

Multi-pane gave writers cognitive management overhead instead of creative flow. The current design is deliberately constrained to two panes:

- **Left pane** — Always the chapter. The unit of work, where you write.
- **Right pane** — Always support. Reference material, wiki slices, notes, timeline, search results.

The support pane *informs*. When you need to *create* or *edit* something substantial, you leave the writing view. The constraint is a feature.

## Technical details

**Stack:** TypeScript, React, TipTap, Dexie (IndexedDB), PostgreSQL

### The undo paradox

Beats exist in two places simultaneously: inside the TipTap editor as highlighted markup, and inside Dexie as structural records. This creates a conflict.

TipTap has an undo stack. Dexie does not.

If a structural action — re-anchoring a Ghost Beat, deduplicating marks, cleaning up orphans — gets added to the undo stack, a writer's `Cmd+Z` can corrupt the relationship between the editor and the database. The editor reverts; the database doesn't.

The fix: pass `addToHistory: false` when applying beat marks from structural actions. The editor is told this isn't a drafting action — don't put it on the undo stack. Drafting actions (the writer typing and selecting) behave normally. Structural actions are invisible to undo.
